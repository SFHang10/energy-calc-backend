Why equipment efficiency against baselines matters

Savings trajectories stay realistic when you can see whether each machine is running as expected versus its operational baseline. Drift away from baseline often shows up on the bill before it shows up in a capital plan—so tracking performance is part of bringing expected costs down, not only funding new equipment.

Spot drift early Usage or demand that sits above baseline for refrigerators, hoods, wok lines, or dishwashers inflates ongoing costs and can distort how “big” your savings opportunity looks elsewhere.
Prioritize the right fixes Comparing live run patterns to baseline helps decide whether tuning, maintenance, or replacement will move the needle first—so spend follows impact.
Anchor forecasts When machinery is running efficiently against baselines, utility and trajectory projections reflect stable operations; that makes upgrade payback and grant timing easier to trust.

Wok To Walk — Warmoesstraat

Rooftop HVAC pack — dining & kitchen load

Live draw sits above the manufacturer mid-load envelope

  • 7-day average during service4.9 kW
  • OEM profile at same OAT + occupancy3.1 kW
  • Supply ΔT vs published design point4.2 K (spec 7 K)

When measured power stays high but delivered ΔT is shallow, the unit is often working harder for less conditioning—coil fouling, economizer or damper issues, or controls fighting the kitchen extract are common causes. That gap shows up in cost before a capital plan does, so it is worth ruling out operations and maintenance first.

Wok battery — gas-fired cooking line

The uplift is not explained by busier service alone: idle time on the line is longer than the baseline assumes, which often points to air-gas mix, partial flame, or pilots/stays lit when they should not be. A combustion check and burner tune typically costs little next to the gas line on the P&L.
