Absolutely — for an equipment specialist in the sustainability arena  should focus on selecting, evaluating, and specifying equipment that performs well not only on energy efficiency, but also on durability, maintenance, lifecycle cost, and broader environmental impact. The UK Government’s Energy Technology List is a strong reference point because it identifies independently verified products that are typically in the top 25% of the market for energy efficiency in their class.

Best brief emphasis
You must balance efficiency, durability, maintainability, and lifecycle value. The best equipment specialist does not just pick the lowest-energy product; they pick the product that will keep performing well, fit the application, and deliver the best long-term sustainability outcome.

Must-knows
UK ETL rules and categories, including how to use the list to identify high-performing equipment and support procurement decisions.

Energy efficiency fundamentals, including how equipment choice affects electricity use, heat loss, operating cost, and carbon emissions.

Lifetime cost thinking, not just purchase price, because efficient equipment often pays back through lower energy and maintenance costs.

Equipment testing and verification, so they can distinguish marketing claims from independently checked performance.

Product fit for purpose, including capacity, duty cycle, operating environment, control compatibility, and ease of servicing.

Build quality and reliability, because a sustainable product should last, be repairable, and avoid premature replacement.

Materials and embodied impact, including whether products are designed with recycled content, fewer hazardous materials, and better end-of-life recyclability.

Maintenance and serviceability, since well-maintained equipment usually performs closer to its design efficiency for longer.

Compatibility with broader sustainability goals, such as monitoring systems, demand control, heat recovery, and integration with building management systems.

Procurement and specification skills, so they can write clear requirements that balance efficiency, quality, resilience, and total value.

Core understandings
ETL is useful because it simplifies the search for efficient equipment and reduces the risk of buying underperforming products.

A sustainable equipment choice should consider the whole lifecycle: manufacture, transport, installation, operation, maintenance, and disposal.

High energy efficiency does not automatically mean the best product if it is unreliable, hard to repair, or poorly matched to the use case.

For sustainability projects, equipment specialists should think about systems interaction, for example how efficient refrigeration or HVAC affects ventilation, heat recovery, and controls.

The best specialists help buyers make decisions that are both practical and future-proof, rather than chasing efficiency alone.

Good role profile
A good job brief would describe someone who can assess equipment options, interpret ETL-style efficiency data, and recommend products that meet both operational and sustainability needs. They should be able to compare suppliers, identify long-term savings, and explain why build quality, repairability, and reliability matter alongside energy performance. In short, the role is about choosing equipment that is efficient, durable, and sensible over its full life, not just “green” on paper.
