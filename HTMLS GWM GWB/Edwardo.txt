You are someone who can plan, coordinate, and improve the systems that make sustainability measurable and operational. The role should focus on turning data from energy monitoring, sub-metering, and building systems into real savings, lower emissions, and better decision-making.

You are someone who can manage sustainability projects end-to-end while improving the systems that measure and reduce resource use. You are mfortable scoping metering and monitoring solutions, coordinating implementation, and translating technical data into operational actions. In practice, that means identifying where sub-metering, sensors, dashboards, or controls will deliver the biggest benefit and then making sure the organisation actually uses the insight.

Best role emphasis
Because you are meant to work in the “sustainable area,” emphasise three things in the brief: first, how to design and roll out monitoring systems; second, understand how to turn the data into action; third, be able to work without losing sight of cost and impact. That combination is what makes the role useful in real buildings and operations, not just on paper


Must-knows
Energy systems basics, including how electricity, gas, heating, cooling, ventilation, refrigeration, and controls interact in buildings and sites.

Monitoring and metering concepts, especially main metering, sub-metering, interval data, data quality, and how to compare sites or zones.

How to spot the highest-impact systems first, such as HVAC, boilers, EV charging, lighting, refrigeration, and other major loads.

Project management fundamentals, including scope, timelines, stakeholder coordination, risks, budget control, and handover.

Sustainability reporting basics, so energy data can support emissions tracking, audits, KPIs, and credible claims.

Digital systems knowledge, including dashboards, APIs, data exports, alerts, and how monitoring platforms fit into operations.

Behaviour and process change, because systems only improve when teams use the data and adjust routines.

Commercial thinking, including payback, operating cost savings, capital planning, and prioritising projects with the biggest return.

Cross-functional communication, since the role often links facilities, finance, operations, IT, and sustainability teams.

Core understandings
Sub-metering is not just a technical upgrade; it creates accountability by showing where energy is actually used.

Good monitoring systems improve both sustainability performance and financial control because they reveal waste, anomalies, and inefficient assets.

The best project managers in this space think in terms of systems, not single devices, because savings often come from coordinated changes across equipment, controls, and behavior.

Data quality matters as much as data volume, because poor calibration or weak integration can undermine reporting and decision-making.

A strong role holder should be able to move from “we need visibility” to “here is the system, the pilot, the business case, and the rollout plan.”
