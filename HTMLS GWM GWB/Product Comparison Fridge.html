Why Choose Low-Energy Equipment?

Low-energy equipment isn't just environmentally friendly—it's a smart financial decision that pays dividends year after year. Modern energy-efficient appliances use up to 50% less electricity than older models, translating to substantial cost savings.

🌍 Environmental Impact

By switching to low-energy equipment, you're not only saving money but also reducing carbon emissions. A single energy-efficient refrigerator can prevent approximately 700kg of CO₂ emissions over 10 years compared to an older model.

📚 Glossary of Terms

Understanding energy efficiency terminology helps make informed decisions.

kWh (Kilowatt-Hour)

A unit of energy equal to one kilowatt (1,000 watts) used for one hour. Your electricity bill is calculated based on kWh consumption. For example, a 100-watt lightbulb running for 10 hours uses 1 kWh.

Energy Efficiency Rating (A-G Scale)

European energy labels rate appliances from A (most efficient) to G (least efficient). Modern standards have been updated, so older A+ ratings may now be C or D class. ETL-listed equipment typically achieves A-C ratings.

ETL (Energy Technology List)

A UK government database of energy-efficient products that meet strict performance criteria. Equipment on the ETL qualifies for Enhanced Capital Allowances, allowing businesses to claim 100% first-year tax relief.

Visit ETL Database →

Variable-Speed Compressor

Modern compressor technology that adjusts speed based on cooling demand, unlike older fixed-speed compressors that run at full power or turn off completely. This reduces energy consumption by 30-50% and extends equipment life.

CO₂ Emissions

Carbon dioxide released when generating electricity. In the UK, each kWh produces approximately 0.5 kg CO₂. Reducing electricity consumption directly reduces your carbon footprint.

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

The complete cost of owning equipment over its lifetime, including purchase price, energy costs, maintenance, and disposal. TCO analysis often reveals that higher initial costs are offset by operational savings.

Payback Period

The time it takes for energy savings to equal the additional upfront cost of efficient equipment. For example, if a low-energy fridge costs £250 more but saves £50/year, the payback period is 5 years.

R-Value / U-Value

Measures of insulation effectiveness. R-value measures resistance to heat flow (higher is better), while U-value measures heat transfer rate (lower is better). Better insulation reduces energy needed for cooling.
