# Show a comparison table of boiler vs boilerless combi ovens consumption

Boilerless combi ovens generally outperform boiler-based models in energy and water efficiency by avoiding constant boiler standby losses and excess water for cooling condensate.

## Boiler vs Boilerless Comparison

| Aspect | Boiler Combi Oven | Boilerless Combi Oven | Savings with Boilerless |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Energy per cycle (steam mode, loaded, 6-10 grid)** | 2.5–4.0 kWh [^5_9] | 1.8–2.7 kWh [^5_9] | 25–40% lower (~1 kWh/cycle) [^5_2][^5_3] |
| **Water per cycle (steam mode, loaded)** | 3–6 L (plus 10–20 L/day boiler cooling) [^5_10][^5_2] | 1.5–3 L (on-demand only) [^5_9][^5_10] | 50–70% lower (~2–4 L/cycle) [^5_2][^5_5] |
| **Idle/standby energy (per hour)** | 0.8–1.5 kW (boiler maintenance) [^5_3] | 0.3–0.6 kW [^5_3] | 50–60% lower [^5_2] |
| **Annual energy (5 cycles/day, 250 days)** | ~4,500–7,500 kWh (£900–1,500/€1,050–1,750) | ~3,000–5,000 kWh (£600–1,000/€700–1,150) | £300–500/€350–600 per year [^5_9][^5_10] |
| **Annual water (same usage)** | ~2,500–5,000 L + boiler top-up | ~1,000–2,000 L | ~1,500–3,000 L/year [^5_10][^5_2] |

Boiler models excel in high-volume pure-steam production but waste energy keeping the boiler hot; boilerless injects water directly onto elements, cutting waste—ideal for most restaurants unless steaming massive loads continuously.[^5_2][^5_3][^5_5]

# Compare lifetime cost savings electric vs gas combi ovens

Lifetime cost savings for combi ovens depend heavily on local energy prices, usage patterns, and maintenance—electric models edge out on efficiency while gas wins where fuel is cheaper, like many UK/EU commercial sites. Over a typical 10-15 year lifespan (common for Rational ovens), gas often delivers 20-40% lower total ownership costs in restaurants despite lower efficiency.[^10_1][^10_5]

## Electric vs Gas Lifetime Cost Comparison

Assumptions: Mid-size 6-10 grid oven (e.g., Rational SCC/iCombi), 5 cycles/day × 250 days/year, UK business rates (Jan 2026: ~20p/kWh electric, ~6p/kWh gas equiv.), 15-year life, moderate maintenance.


| Cost Component | Electric (e.g., SCC 101 E) | Gas (e.g., SCC 201 G) | Net Savings: Gas Advantage |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Upfront Purchase** | £12,000–£18,000 | £14,000–£22,000 (10-20% higher) [^10_5] | Gas: -£2,000 (higher initial) |
| **Annual Energy** (3,750 cycles, ~2.5 kWh or 10 kWh gas equiv./cycle) | £1,875 (~4,700 kWh × 20p, 85% efficient) [^10_1] | £1,125 (~18,750 kWh gas equiv. × 6p, 70% efficient) [^10_1][^10_2] | Gas: £750/year (£11,250 over 15 years) |
| **Annual Maintenance** | £200–£300 (elements, simpler) | £350–£500 (burners, venting) [^10_1][^10_5] | Electric: £150/year (£2,250 over 15 years) |
| **Installation** | £1,000–£2,000 (electrical upgrades) | £2,500–£4,000 (gas line + venting) [^10_5] | Gas: -£1,500 (higher) |
| **Total 15-Year Cost** | £37,500–£48,000 | £35,000–£46,000 | **Gas saves £2,500–£6,000 overall** [^10_1][^10_5] |

Electric shines in low-use or high-electricity-cost areas with simpler upkeep; gas pulls ahead for busy restaurants (faster recovery, lower fuel bills). Factor your NL site's rates—gas typically cheaper for high-volume cooking.[^10_2][^10_6]
