# [https://etl.energysecurity.gov.uk/product-search/product/86411](https://etl.energysecurity.gov.uk/product-search/product/86411)

This ETL-listed product is a high‑efficiency, converter‑fed asynchronous IE4 electric motor designed for use with a variable speed drive (VSD) in industrial or commercial applications such as pumps, fans, conveyors and general drive systems. Because it meets the UK Energy Technology List (ETL) criteria, it sits within roughly the top 25% of the market for energy efficiency in its class, so it can materially cut electricity use and running costs compared with a standard motor.[^1][^4]

## What the product is

- The product is an **asynchronous** (induction) IE4 motor rated at 0.55 kW output, 4‑pole, 230 V, with a multi‑phase supply and designed to operate with an electronic VSD.[^1]
- It is listed under the ETL technology group “Motors, Drives \& Fans” and the subtechnology “Converter‑fed motors (sold without VSD),” with a measured combined motor‑and‑drive efficiency of 84.1% using BS EN 60034‑2‑1:2014 Method 2‑1‑2A.[^4][^1]
- The motor is manufactured by NORD Gear Ltd and falls under Uniclass codes for alternating‑current squirrel‑cage and wound‑rotor induction motors, which helps specifiers integrate it into BIM and procurement systems.[^4][^1]


## How it is typically used

In practice, this motor is installed as part of a drive train, almost always paired with a compatible VSD:

- **Typical applications**
    - Pumps and fans where flow or pressure varies (HVAC, industrial processes, water systems).
    - Conveyors, mixers, small machine drives and other continuous or intermittent duty loads.
    - Situations where speed control, soft starting and process optimisation are important (for example, variable‑flow pumping or variable‑speed air handling).
- **Functional set‑up (conceptual block diagram – text form)**
    - Power supply (three‑phase, 230 V) → VSD (rectifier, DC link, inverter) → Converter‑fed IE4 motor → Mechanical load (e.g. pump impeller or fan).
    - Control layer: sensors (pressure, flow, speed, temperature) → controller/PLC/BMS → VSD speed command → motor torque and speed.

Because the motor is optimised for converter operation, it is intended to run over a range of speeds rather than just at fixed mains frequency, which is what enables the largest energy savings on variable‑torque loads.[^1][^4]

## Why and where it saves energy

Energy savings come from both the high intrinsic motor efficiency and, more importantly, from pairing it with a VSD to match output to demand:

- **High motor‑plus‑drive efficiency**
    - The combined motor and drive efficiency of 84.1% at the rated point is significantly higher than that of many legacy motors and simple on/off controls, especially older IE1/IE2 motors that might sit in the 70–80% range at similar sizes.[^4][^1]
    - Because losses (heat in windings, iron losses, stray load losses) are lower, less electrical input is needed for the same shaft power.
- **Variable speed vs. throttling**
    - In a traditional system, a fixed‑speed motor runs at full speed and output is controlled by dampers, valves or bypasses, which wastes energy as pressure or flow is “thrown away.”
    - With a VSD and high‑efficiency converter‑fed motor, the control strategy is to reduce motor speed instead; for variable‑torque loads, power roughly follows the cube of speed, so even modest speed reductions cut electrical consumption sharply.
- **Soft‑start and optimised operation**
    - Soft starting reduces inrush currents and mechanical stress, which can cut peak‑demand charges and extend equipment life.
    - The ability to fine‑tune speed allows systems to operate closer to optimal efficiency points (for example, matching pump head to system curve), avoiding over‑pumping or over‑ventilation.


## Illustrative energy and cost saving example

The exact saving depends on your existing motor, operating profile, tariff and control strategy, but a simple comparison illustrates the potential:

- Suppose a legacy 0.55 kW motor (older IE1/IE2) runs 8 hours a day, 5 days a week, at an effective efficiency of about 75% in a fixed‑speed on/off setup.
- Replacing it with this 0.55 kW IE4 converter‑fed motor and an appropriate VSD, operated so that the average speed is reduced by 15–20% on a variable‑torque load (for example, fan or pump at part‑load most of the time) can plausibly reduce annual electricity use by tens of percent versus the old arrangement.
- Because the motor is ETL‑listed, you also gain confidence that it has been independently verified to meet strict performance criteria and sits among the most efficient options in its class, which reduces the risk of over‑stated savings claims and supports business‑case approval.[^7][^1][^4]

In many commercial and industrial sites, motors of this size run for long hours in large numbers; incremental efficiency gains compounded across the fleet and combined with variable‑speed control can deliver meaningful reductions in total electricity consumption and operating cost over the equipment lifetime.[^5][^4]
