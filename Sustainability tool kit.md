## Core sustainability concepts

* **Sustainability**
Meeting today’s needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs—balancing environmental protection, social fairness and economic viability.[^1\_1](https://insights.ehl.edu/how-to-make-restaurant-green)
* **Triple bottom line (People, Planet, Profit)**
A framework that measures success across social impact, environmental impact and financial performance—not just profit alone.[^1\_1](https://www.kikkoman.eu/food-service/inspiration/detail/5-tips-for-greater-sustainability-in-your-restaurant-and-the-food-service-industry)
* **Circular economy**
Designing out waste by keeping materials and food in use longer (reuse, repair, repurpose, recycle) and regenerating natural systems. Highly relevant for packaging, food scraps and kitchen design.\[^1\_3]\[^1\_4]
* **Regenerative practices**
Going beyond “doing less harm” to actively improving ecosystems—e.g., sourcing from farms that rebuild soil health and biodiversity.[^1\_3](https://luxiders.com/best-restaurants-in-europe-with-innovative-sustainable-concepts/)



## Climate and carbon

* **Carbon footprint**
The total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions caused directly and indirectly by your restaurant (cooking, energy, deliveries, waste, purchased ingredients).[^1\_5](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **CO₂e (carbon dioxide equivalent)**
A common unit that expresses all GHGs (like methane and nitrous oxide) as the equivalent amount of CO₂, so you can add them up.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions**
Scope 1: direct emissions you control (e.g., gas boilers, delivery vans you own).
Scope 2: emissions from purchased electricity/heat.
Scope 3: everything else in your value chain (ingredients, packaging, waste, staff travel). For restaurants, Scope 3 is usually the biggest chunk.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **Science-based targets (SBTi-style)**
Emission-reduction goals aligned with climate science (e.g., 25–30% cut by 2030) to stay within safe global warming limits.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)

**Net zero**
Reducing emissions as much as possible and balancing any remaining ones with high-quality removals; for restaurants this usually means deep cuts first, then offsets only as a last resort.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)


Scope 3 emissions for restaurants

* **Scope 3 emissions**
All the greenhouse gas emissions linked to your restaurant that happen outside your own building and direct fuel use, including ingredients, packaging, deliveries, waste, staff travel and supplier activities. For restaurants, this is usually the largest share of total emissions.[^1\_5](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Purchased goods and services**
Emissions from the food, drink, cleaning products and other supplies you buy.
**How to reduce it:** buy more plant-based ingredients, reduce high-impact meats and dairy, choose seasonal and local products, work with suppliers that can share carbon data, and design menus to use ingredients more efficiently.\[^1\_1]\[^1\_5]
* **Menu carbon footprint**
The emissions linked to each dish or menu item, based on ingredients, portions and preparation.
**How to reduce it:** compare dishes, highlight lower-carbon options, resize portions, and swap ingredients with lower-impact alternatives where taste and quality are preserved.[^1\_1](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Supplier emissions**
The emissions created before ingredients reach you, such as farming, processing and manufacturing.
**How to reduce it:** ask suppliers for emissions information, prefer suppliers with renewable energy or regenerative farming practices, and build sustainability expectations into purchasing decisions.[^1\_5](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Packaging emissions**
Emissions from making bottles, cartons, boxes, trays, cups and takeaway containers.
**How to reduce it:** cut unnecessary packaging, switch to reusable or returnable systems where possible, and choose lightweight, recycled or recyclable materials.[^1\_5](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Upstream transport and distribution**
Emissions from moving goods from farms and factories to your restaurant.
**How to reduce it:** consolidate deliveries, buy from closer suppliers where practical, improve ordering to avoid rush shipments, and work with distributors that use lower-emission vehicles or better route planning.\[^1\_5]
* **Food waste**
Food bought or prepared that is not eaten and ends up discarded. It causes emissions in production and again in disposal.
**How to reduce it:** forecast demand better, track waste by station, improve storage, use nose-to-tail and leaf-to-root cooking, adjust portion sizes, and donate safe surplus food where local rules allow.\[^1\_1]\[^1\_5]
* **Waste treatment**
What happens to food waste and packaging after disposal, such as composting, anaerobic digestion, recycling or landfill.
**How to reduce it:** separate waste correctly, increase recycling and organics collection, avoid contamination, and reduce the amount of waste created in the first place.\[^1\_1]\[^1\_5]
* **Business travel and commuting**
Emissions from staff travel to work, supplier meetings, events and off-site activity.
**How to reduce it:** support public transport, cycling and car sharing, reduce unnecessary trips, use video meetings where possible, and group visits into fewer journeys.\[^1\_5]
* **Delivery emissions**
Emissions from third-party deliveries, takeaway logistics and catering transport.
**How to reduce it:** use route-efficient delivery partners, prefer bikes or electric vehicles in cities, reduce failed deliveries, and limit packaging and transport distance where possible.\[^1\_1]\[^1\_5]
* **Capital goods**
Emissions from producing ovens, fridges, dishwashers, furniture and fit-out materials. These are often one-off but significant.
**How to reduce it:** repair before replacing, buy durable equipment, extend equipment life, and choose efficient products that reduce lifetime emissions.[^1\_1](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Scope 3 hotspot**
The part of your value chain with the biggest emissions impact, often beef, dairy, food waste, packaging or delivery.
**How to reduce it:** focus first on the biggest hotspot, because that usually gives the fastest and most meaningful cuts.[^1\_5](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Supplier engagement**
Working with suppliers to improve data, reduce emissions and improve sourcing practices.
**How to reduce it:** ask for carbon reporting, set simple expectations, reward progress in procurement, and build sustainability questions into tenders and contracts.[^1\_5](https://www.un.org/en/actnow/ten-actions)
* **Data gaps**
Missing or incomplete emissions information from suppliers or products.
**How to reduce it:** start with estimates, improve data quality over time, request product-level information, and focus on the categories that matter most first.\[^1\_5]



## Practical message for users

For restaurants, the biggest Scope 3 reductions usually come from **changing what is bought, how much is wasted, and how products move through the supply chain**. Small operational changes help, but the biggest gains usually come from menu design, supplier choices and waste prevention.\[^1\_1]\[^1\_5]

## Food and menu

* **Farm-to-table / local and seasonal sourcing**
Buying ingredients close to where you operate and in season to cut transport emissions, support local producers and improve freshness.[^1\_2](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Plant-forward / plant-based**
Menus designed to increase the share of plants (vegetables, legumes, grains) and reduce reliance on animal products, especially high-impact meats.[^1\_3](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **Protein shift (e.g., 60:40 split)**
A target to serve more plant protein than animal protein (for example 60% plant, 40% animal) across your menu, aligned with WWF/planetary health guidance.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **Low-carbon dishes / CO₂ threshold per dish**
Designing menu items to stay under a set emissions limit (e.g., many main dishes below 900 g CO₂e) and labelling them to guide customer choices.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **Nose-to-tail and leaf-to-root cooking**
Using the whole animal or plant (offcuts, stems, peels) to minimise waste and get more value from each ingredient.[^1\_1](https://www.kikkoman.eu/food-service/inspiration/detail/5-tips-for-greater-sustainability-in-your-restaurant-and-the-food-service-industry)
* **Hyper-seasonal menu**
Menus that change frequently (weekly/daily) based on what’s actually in season and available locally, reducing storage, transport and waste.[^1\_3](https://luxiders.com/best-restaurants-in-europe-with-innovative-sustainable-concepts/)



## Waste and materials

* **Food waste**
Any food that is lost or discarded along the supply chain or in your kitchen/service (pre-consumer prep waste, plate waste, spoilage). A major emissions and cost issue.[^1\_4](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Food surplus / rescue**
Safe, edible food that would otherwise be wasted but can be donated to food banks, charities or staff, following local rules.\[^1\_6]\[^1\_4]
* **Composting**
Turning organic waste (peelings, coffee grounds, unserved food where allowed) into soil amendment, either on-site or via a collection service.[^1\_6](https://luxiders.com/best-restaurants-in-europe-with-innovative-sustainable-concepts/)
* **Anaerobic digestion (AD)**
An industrial process that breaks down organic waste to produce biogas (energy) and digestate (fertiliser); common in UK/EU waste systems.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Zero-waste restaurant**
An operation designed to send as little as possible to landfill/incineration by preventing waste, reusing materials, composting and recycling; often includes making ingredients in-house and composting residuals.[^1\_3](https://luxiders.com/best-restaurants-in-europe-with-innovative-sustainable-concepts/)
* **Single-use plastics (SUPs)**
Items like plastic cutlery, plates, straws and some takeaway containers. Many are banned or restricted in the UK and EU, pushing venues to reusable or alternative materials.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Reusable / returnable packaging**
Systems where customers or delivery partners use returnable containers (e.g., boxes, cups) that are cleaned and reused, cutting single-use waste.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **EU Ecolabel / Safer Choice-type labels**
Certifications showing cleaning products, paper goods or other supplies meet strict environmental and health criteria.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)



## Energy, water and buildings

* **Renewable energy**
Electricity or heat from sources like wind, solar or hydro; switching suppliers or adding on-site solar is a quick win for many restaurants.[^1\_1](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Energy efficiency**
Using less energy to do the same work—e.g., LED lighting, efficient fridges/ovens, good insulation, smart controls. Cuts both bills and emissions.[^1\_2](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Water efficiency**
Reducing water use with low-flow taps/toilets, fixing leaks, running dishwashers only when full, and training staff on water-saving practices.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Green building / circular design**
Designing or refurbishing restaurant spaces with sustainable materials, energy-efficient systems and layouts that reduce waste and enable reuse/recycling.[^1\_2](https://luxiders.com/best-restaurants-in-europe-with-innovative-sustainable-concepts/)



## Supply chain and governance

* **Ethical sourcing**
Buying from suppliers that treat workers fairly, pay living wages, follow animal welfare standards and protect the environment.[^1\_2](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Short supply chains**
Buying directly from local producers or via short intermediaries to increase transparency, reduce transport and support local economies.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive)**
An EU rule requiring certain companies (and parts of their value chains) to report on environmental, social and governance impacts; affects larger groups and some suppliers to big brands.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance)**
The three pillars used by investors and regulators to assess sustainability performance; for restaurants this covers emissions, labour practices, diversity, food safety, etc.\[^1\_4]
* **Food Made Good Standard (SRA)**
A hospitality-specific framework from the Sustainable Restaurant Association that defines “good” practice across sourcing, environment, society and governance, with ratings for restaurants.[^1\_7](https://touringproject.eu/best-practice/the-sra-the-sustainable-restaurant-association/)
* **Michelin Green Star**
A Michelin recognition for restaurants with outstanding sustainability practices (e.g., local sourcing, waste reduction, community engagement).[^1\_3](https://luxiders.com/best-restaurants-in-europe-with-innovative-sustainable-concepts/)



## Certifications and labels you may see

* **Organic**
Food produced without synthetic pesticides or fertilisers, with strict rules on animal welfare and processing; certified by national/EU bodies.[^1\_1](https://www.kikkoman.eu/food-service/inspiration/detail/5-tips-for-greater-sustainability-in-your-restaurant-and-the-food-service-industry)
* **Fairtrade**
A label ensuring producers (often in the Global South) receive fair prices and work under better social and environmental conditions (common for coffee, tea, chocolate, sugar).[^1\_1](https://www.kikkoman.eu/food-service/inspiration/detail/5-tips-for-greater-sustainability-in-your-restaurant-and-the-food-service-industry)
* **Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) / Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC)**
Labels for wild-caught and farmed seafood that meet sustainability standards on fish stocks, habitats and management.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Rainforest Alliance / similar**
Certifications for crops like coffee and cocoa that address biodiversity, climate and farmer livelihoods.[^1\_1](https://www.kikkoman.eu/food-service/inspiration/detail/5-tips-for-greater-sustainability-in-your-restaurant-and-the-food-service-industry)



## Customer-facing concepts and behaviours

* **Sustainable menu labelling**
Clear signs or icons showing low-carbon, plant-based, local/seasonal or certified items to help guests choose more sustainably.[^1\_5](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Meatless Monday / plant-focused promotions**
Campaigns that highlight meat-free dishes on specific days or periods to normalise lower-impact eating without removing choice.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)
* **Portion control / right-sized portions**
Offering flexible or smaller portions and encouraging take-home of leftovers to reduce plate waste while maintaining satisfaction.[^1\_2](https://www.kikkoman.eu/food-service/inspiration/detail/5-tips-for-greater-sustainability-in-your-restaurant-and-the-food-service-industry)
* **Eco-friendly delivery options**
Using e-bikes, electric vehicles, route optimisation and incentives for off-peak or nearby orders to cut delivery emissions.[^1\_6](https://www.lightspeedhq.com/uk/blog/restaurant-sustainability-guide-reduce-your-carbon-footprint/)



## Metrics and tracking

* **Key performance indicators (KPIs) for sustainability**
Simple measures like: kg food waste per 100 covers, % plant-based dishes sold, kWh energy per cover, litres water per cover, % suppliers with certifications.[^1\_2](https://insights.ehl.edu/how-to-make-restaurant-green)
* **Carbon accounting for menus**
Estimating emissions per dish or per menu using recognised methods, then using that data to set targets and redesign recipes.[^1\_5](https://eaternity.org/solutions/restaurants)
* **Transparency and reporting**
Sharing progress (and challenges) with staff and guests through dashboards, menu notes or short updates—building trust and engagement.[^1\_2](https://insights.ehl.edu/how-to-make-restaurant-green)

## One‑Page Glossary: Scope 3 Emissions for Restaurants (EU/UK)

**Scope 3 emissions (for restaurants)** – All the greenhouse gas emissions linked to your restaurant that happen *outside* your own kitchen and building. They come from your supply chain: growing and making your ingredients, packaging, deliveries, waste treatment, staff travel, etc. For most restaurants, 90–99% of total emissions are Scope 3.[^2\_1](https://www.carbonmaps.io/blog/tackling-scopes-1-2-and-3-in-food-service)[^2\_3](https://www.foodsteps.earth/post/how-can-food-service-and-manufacturers-measure-and-cut-scope-3-emissions-the-answer-is-data)

\---

### Upstream Scope 3 (before food reaches your kitchen)

* **Purchased goods \& services (ingredients)**
Emissions from producing the food and drink you buy: fertilisers and feed on farms, energy in factories, land-use change (e.g. deforestation for soy or beef), and processing into cheese, chocolate, ready sauces, etc. Usually the biggest Scope 3 source for restaurants.[^2\_2](https://www.foodsteps.earth/post/how-can-food-service-and-manufacturers-measure-and-cut-scope-3-emissions-the-answer-is-data)[^2\_4](https://thesra.org/media/bl5pzscn/understanding-scope-3-in-hospitality.pdf)
* **Purchased packaging materials**
Emissions from making the boxes, trays, bottles, cans, cartons, takeaway containers and labels you use. Includes extraction of raw materials (paper, plastic, glass, metal) and manufacturing energy.[^2\_4](https://thesra.org/media/bl5pzscn/understanding-scope-3-in-hospitality.pdf)
* **Capital goods (equipment \& fit‑out)**
Emissions from producing ovens, fridges, dishwashers, furniture, kitchen tools, and building/renovation materials. These are “upfront” emissions that you benefit from over years.[^2\_4](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)
* **Upstream transport \& distribution (to you)**
Emissions from moving ingredients and packaging from farms and factories to processors, distributors and finally to your restaurant (by road, rail, air or sea). Includes cold storage during transport.[^2\_2](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)
* **Waste generated in operations (upstream part)**
Emissions linked to making products that later become your waste (e.g. unserved food, spoiled deliveries, packaging offcuts) before they even reach your bin.[^2\_2](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)
* **Business travel \& employee commuting**
Emissions from staff and managers travelling to and from work, and any work-related travel (trains, flights, cars, buses). Often a smaller but visible part of Scope 3.[^2\_3](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)

\---

### Downstream Scope 3 (after food leaves your kitchen)

* **Downstream transport \& delivery to customers**
Emissions from third‑party delivery services, couriers or distributors taking your food to customers (e.g. takeaway, meal kits, catering events). Includes delivery vans, bikes and any associated logistics.[^2\_4](https://www.carbonmaps.io/blog/tackling-scopes-1-2-and-3-in-food-service)
* **Use of sold products (if relevant)**
Emissions from how customers store or reheat your food (e.g. home freezing, microwaving) or use your products (like coffee capsules). More relevant if you sell retail or ready‑to‑cook products.[^2\_4](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)
* **End‑of‑life treatment of sold products**
Emissions from what happens to your packaging and food waste after customers finish: landfill, incineration, recycling, composting or anaerobic digestion, including the energy used in those processes.[^2\_2](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)
* **Franchises \& leased operations (if applicable)**
If you run or own franchised restaurants or leased sites, their day‑to‑day emissions may count as your Scope 3 (depending on how you set your boundaries).[^2\_4](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)

### Key related terms

* **Scope 1 \& 2 vs Scope 3**
Scope 1: direct emissions you control (e.g. gas boilers, owned vans).
Scope 2: emissions from purchased electricity/heat.
Scope 3: everything else in your value chain. For restaurants, Scope 3 usually dwarfs Scopes 1 and 2.[^2\_3](https://www.carbonmaps.io/blog/tackling-scopes-1-2-and-3-in-food-service)[^2\_4](https://klimato.com/klimato-insights/examples-of-scope-1-2-and-3-emissions-in-food-businesses)
* **Purchased goods \& services category (GHG Protocol)**
The main Scope 3 category for restaurants, covering all bought ingredients, beverages, packaging and many services. Often targeted first for reductions.[^2\_5](https://www.scribd.com/document/847436068/Scope-3-GHG-Measurement-Reporting-Protocols-Sector-Guidance-for-Food-Drink-Businesses)[^2\_3](https://www.foodsteps.earth/post/how-can-food-service-and-manufacturers-measure-and-cut-scope-3-emissions-the-answer-is-data)
* **Food Steps / carbon per dish**
Methods that estimate the carbon footprint of individual dishes based on ingredients and portions; used to identify hotspots in your menu’s Scope 3 emissions (e.g. dough, beef, dairy).[^2\_3](https://www.foodsteps.earth/post/how-can-food-service-and-manufacturers-measure-and-cut-scope-3-emissions-the-answer-is-data)
* **Supplier engagement**
Working with your suppliers (farmers, processors, distributors) to share data, set targets and improve practices (e.g. more plant-based products, better packaging, renewable energy on farms). Essential to cut Scope 3.[^2\_1](https://thesra.org/media/bl5pzscn/understanding-scope-3-in-hospitality.pdf)
* **Science‑based targets for Scope 3**
Emission‑reduction goals aligned with climate science that include your supply chain (e.g. cutting purchased food emissions in line with a 1.5°C pathway).[^2\_6](https://www.wrap.ngo/resources/guide/scope-3-ghg-measurement-and-reporting-protocols-food-and-drink)
* **Data gaps \& estimation**
Because many suppliers don’t yet report detailed emissions, restaurants often use averages, spend-based data or industry factors to estimate Scope 3. Improving data quality over time is part of the journey.[^2\_7](https://www.wrap.ngo/resources/guide/scope-3-ghg-measurement-and-reporting-protocols-food-and-drink)[^2\_3](https://www.foodsteps.earth/post/how-can-food-service-and-manufacturers-measure-and-cut-scope-3-emissions-the-answer-is-data)
